Introduction

Bacteria do not move randomly. They sense chemical gradients in their environment and bias their movement toward nutrients and away from toxins — a process called chemotaxis. Understanding chemotaxis matters for human health because it governs how gut bacteria colonise the intestine, how pathogens spread through tissue, and how bacterial communities respond to antibiotics.

The Keller-Segel (KS) model is the standard mathematical description of chemotaxis. It treats the bacterial population as a density field that diffuses randomly but also drifts up chemical gradients. The model was originally developed for dilute populations, but real biological systems often involve very high bacterial densities — for example in the gut, in biofilms, or during infection.

This project uses the dataset from Phan et al. (2024), who measured bacterial travelling waves in a microfluidic channel at four different initial densities. We first test whether the original KS model can explain the data, then introduce a density-dependent correction that fixes its failure at high density. Finally, motivated by recent work on suicidal chemotaxis (Oliveira et al. 2022), we extend the model to include an antibiotic field and ask what happens when bacteria are attracted toward a lethal signal.

The connection to diet and health is direct: the gut microbiome is a dense bacterial community in which chemotaxis shapes spatial organisation, nutrient competition, and the response to antimicrobial compounds. A model that correctly describes density-dependent chemotaxis is therefore a step toward understanding how diet-driven changes in gut bacterial density affect community behaviour and antibiotic susceptibility.

Methods

Experimental data

We used the processed dataset from Phan et al. (2024), which contains mean bacterial density profiles B(z) and aspartate concentration profiles A(z) for four density groups: LOW (~0.1–0.3 OD), MID (~3.0–3.3 OD), HIGH (~6.2–6.7 OD), and EXT (~7.6–8.3 OD). The coordinate z is in the co-moving frame, so z = 0 always marks the peak of the bacterial band. All profiles were measured in a microfluidic channel 6 mm long.

The Keller-Segel model

The standard KS model describes the time evolution of bacterial density B and aspartate concentration A:

∂B/∂t = Db ∂²B/∂z² − ∂/∂z [χ · B · ∂Φ/∂z] + α B

∂A/∂t = DA ∂²A/∂z² − γ · B · A / (A + Ath)

Φ = ln(1 + A / Ki)

The first equation says bacteria spread by diffusion (Db term), drift up the perceived food gradient (χ term), and grow at rate α. The second equation says aspartate diffuses (DA term) and is consumed by bacteria following Michaelis-Menten kinetics. The perceived signal Φ is logarithmic, reflecting Weber's law: bacteria are more sensitive to gradients when background concentration is low.

Density-dependent correction

Following Phan et al. (2024), we replaced the constant chemotactic sensitivity χ with a density-dependent form:

χ(B) = χ0 / (1 + B / Bth)

This means crowded bacteria respond less strongly to the food gradient. The parameter Bth ≈ 8.8 OD is the density at which sensitivity is halved. At low density (B ≪ Bth) the model reduces to the original KS equation.

Antibiotic extension

We added a third variable C(z,t) for antibiotic concentration and a second chemotaxis term pointing toward the antibiotic:

∂B/∂t = Db ∂²B/∂z² − ∂/∂z [χA(B) · B · ∂ΦA/∂z] − ∂/∂z [χC(B,C) · B · ∂ΦC/∂z] + α B − μ(C) B

∂C/∂t = DC ∂²C/∂z² − κ B C

The killing rate μ(C) activates above the minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC). The antibiotic chemotaxis term χC is motivated by Oliveira et al. (2022), who showed that Pseudomonas aeruginosa actively swims toward antibiotics. We applied this behaviour to our E. coli model as a theoretical exploration.

Numerical implementation

All PDEs were solved using explicit Euler time-stepping with central finite differences in space. The experimental profiles were interpolated onto a coarse grid of 300 points (dz ≈ 0.02 mm) for numerical stability. The time step was chosen to satisfy the stability condition dt < dz² / (2D). No-flux boundary conditions were applied at both ends of the channel.

Results and Discussion

The original KS model with constant χ fitted the LOW density data well but predicted a band that was too narrow and sharp at HIGH and EXT density. This failure was confirmed by a rate-of-change diagnostic: the model's right-hand side was much larger than the measured ∂B/∂t at high density, showing that the model was over-driving the chemotaxis term.

Replacing constant χ with the density-dependent form χ(B) = χ0 / (1 + B/Bth) fixed the model at all four density groups simultaneously. The wide flat band at high density was reproduced, and the narrow sharp band at low density was preserved. This is a strong result: one additional parameter (Bth) unifies the behaviour across a 50-fold range of bacterial density.

Forward simulation from the real experimental initial conditions reproduced the correct wave speed (~0.28 mm/min) and band shape over five minutes. The aspartate profile evolved as expected, with depletion behind the band and fresh nutrient ahead — the self-sustaining mechanism that drives the travelling wave.

The antibiotic extension showed that bacteria attracted toward an antibiotic above the MIC enter the killing zone and are eliminated, disrupting the travelling wave. An important finding was that dense colonies were harder to disrupt: because crowding already reduces χ(B), high-density bacteria are less strongly attracted to the antibiotic gradient and fewer enter the killing zone. This suggests that the density-dependent suppression of chemotaxis, originally identified as a response to crowding in the food gradient, may also inadvertently protect high-density colonies from suicidal antibiotic attraction.

These results have implications for gut health. The gut microbiome operates at very high bacterial densities, where our model predicts that chemotactic responses are substantially reduced. This could mean that gut bacteria are less responsive to nutrient gradients than dilute laboratory cultures suggest, and that antibiotic-driven disruption of chemotaxis may be density-dependent — potentially explaining why high-density biofilms are harder to treat.

Results

What the model tells us

Result 1 — Experimental data

Bacteria form a moving band — but the shape changes with density

Bacteria were placed in a channel filled with aspartate (a nutrient they like). They swam toward it, ate it, and formed a travelling band. We measured the shape of this band at four different starting densities.

At LOW density the band is narrow and sharp. At HIGH and EXT density the band is much wider and flatter. The band still moves at roughly the same speed in all cases (~0.28 mm/min), but its shape is very different. This is the key observation we need our model to explain.

Result 2 — Original model

The standard model works at low density but gets it wrong at high density

The Keller-Segel model describes how bacteria spread out randomly and also move toward food. We fitted it to the LOW density data and it matched well. But when we used the same settings for HIGH and EXT density, the model predicted a narrow sharp band — not the wide flat one we actually see.

The model is too aggressive: it assumes bacteria always chase the food gradient just as hard, no matter how crowded they are. The data show that is not true.

This check compares what the model predicts should be happening to what is actually measured. At HIGH and EXT density the model is far too large — confirming it over-drives the chemotaxis.

Result 3 — Fixed model

One small change fixes the model for all four densities

We changed one thing: instead of bacteria always chasing food at the same strength, we let that strength decrease when the bacteria are crowded. When there are lots of bacteria packed together, each one responds less strongly to the food gradient.

This single change fixes the model at all four densities at once. The wide flat band at high density is now reproduced, and the narrow sharp band at low density is still correct. One formula, four density groups, all matching.

With the fix applied, the model now matches the measured rate of change at all densities.

Result 4 — Running the model forward in time

The model correctly predicts how the band moves over time

We took the real measured profiles as a starting point and ran the model forward for five minutes. The bacterial band moved to the right at about 0.28 mm/min — matching the speed seen in the experiment.

Behind the band, the food gets eaten up. Ahead of the band, fresh food remains. This is exactly what keeps the band moving: bacteria always have food to chase in front of them and nothing left behind them. The model captures this correctly.

Result 5 — What happens when antibiotics are added?

Bacteria attracted toward antibiotics get killed — but dense colonies are harder to disrupt

Some bacteria have been shown to swim toward antibiotics rather than away from them. We added this behaviour to our model and placed an antibiotic source ahead of the bacterial band.

Below a certain concentration the bacteria barely notice the antibiotic and the band moves normally. Above that level, bacteria at the front swim into the antibiotic zone and get killed. The band slows down and starts to break apart.

Interestingly, at high density the bacteria are less attracted to the antibiotic — because crowding already reduces how strongly they respond to any gradient. A dense colony is actually harder to disrupt this way.

The bacterial band moves right until it hits the antibiotic zone, where bacteria are killed and the band breaks down.
