Why this model matters

The project in one picture

1. Original Keller-Segel

Bacteria are treated as a density field. They diffuse randomly but also move up chemical gradients they perceive as beneficial.

2. Phan et al. correction

We used the Phan et al. dataset to validate the model and test where the original Keller-Segel description breaks down at high density.

3. Our extension

We add an antibiotic field and ask what happens when bacteria experience both food attraction and a harmful but attractive signal.

Equation breakdown

Each term has a biological meaning

Random spreading

Db∇²B

Even without signals, bacteria wander. This smooths out sharp peaks.

Food attraction

-∇·(χAB∇ΦA)

Bacteria drift toward richer food regions, which can concentrate the population.

Antibiotic attraction

-∇·(χCB∇ΦC)

Our extension asks what if bacteria also move toward a harmful signal.

Birth and death

+ αB - μ(C)B

Growth adds cells. High antibiotic raises the death rate and can destroy clusters.

Project workflow

How the whole project fits together

Step 1

Visualise the dataset

Load bacterial and aspartate profiles from the Phan et al. processed data.

Step 2

Test the original KS model

Evaluate how well the standard chemotaxis equations explain the measured profiles.

Step 3

Add density dependence

Let chemotaxis and attractant consumption weaken at high density, following Phan et al.

Step 4

Forward simulation

Run the corrected PDE model and compare the evolving wave to experiment.

Step 5

Antibiotic extension

Introduce a second signal field so food attraction competes with antibiotic-driven mortality.

Interactive mini-game

Bacteria chemotaxis game

This game is a lighter, more playful interaction than the full KS playground. Click inside the canvas to drop food. The bacteria first wander randomly, then begin to bias their motion toward the food source.

It is not a full PDE solver, but it still reflects three core KS ideas: diffusion, delayed gradient sensing, and density-dependent sensitivity.
