Equation breakdown

Random spreading

Db∇²B

Even without signals, bacteria wander. This smooths out sharp peaks.

Food attraction

-∇·(χAB∇ΦA)

Bacteria drift toward richer food regions, which can concentrate the population.

Antibiotic attraction

-∇·(χCB∇ΦC)

Our extension asks what if bacteria also move toward a harmful signal.

Birth and death

+ αB - μ(C)B

Growth adds cells. High antibiotic raises the death rate and can destroy clusters.

Results

What the model tells us

Result 1 — Experimental data

Bacterial waves broaden at high density

Mean bacterial density B(z) and aspartate concentration A(z) profiles for the four experimental groups (LOW → EXT). Shaded bands show ±1 standard deviation. The coordinate z is in the co-moving frame, so z = 0 always marks the bacterial peak.

At LOW density the band is narrow and sharply peaked; the aspartate profile shows a clean depletion zone behind the wave. As density increases the peak broadens and flattens. At EXT density the band is roughly twice as wide, and aspartate depletion behind the wave is less complete. This broadening is the central experimental observation the original model cannot reproduce.

Result 2 — Original Keller-Segel model

Constant χ fits LOW density but fails at HIGH and EXT

The original KS model (dashed orange) uses a constant chemotactic sensitivity χ = 0.0033 mm²/s fitted to the LOW density data. It gives an excellent fit at LOW density but systematically over-predicts band sharpness at higher densities, producing a narrow tall peak where the data show a broad flat distribution.

The rate-of-change diagnostic (below) confirms the problem: the model’s right-hand side is much larger in magnitude than the measured ∂B/∂t at HIGH and EXT density, pointing directly to χ as the parameter that needs correcting.

Rate-of-change diagnostic: model RHS (orange) vs measured ∂B/∂t (blue).

Result 3 — Modified Keller-Segel model

Density-dependent χ(B) fixes all four groups simultaneously

Replacing constant χ with χ(B) = χ0 / (1 + B/Bth) dramatically improves the fit at all densities (green dashed line). Crowded bacteria follow the gradient less aggressively because their effective sensitivity is reduced. The half-saturation density Bth ≈ 8.8 OD means that at EXT density the chemotactic sensitivity is roughly halved compared to the dilute limit.

Crucially, the LOW density fit is preserved: when B « Bth the correction factor is close to 1 and the model reduces to the original KS equation. The density-dependent term unifies behaviour across all four conditions with a single parameter set.

Modified model RHS (green) now closely tracks measured ∂B/∂t at all densities.

Result 4 — Forward PDE simulation

The model predicts realistic wave propagation from real initial conditions

Starting from the experimentally measured LOW density profiles, the modified model produces a self-sustaining travelling wave. Over five minutes the bacterial band moves approximately 1.4 mm to the right, consistent with the measured wave speed of ~0.28 mm/min for the LOW density group.

The aspartate profile evolves as expected: the region behind the wave becomes progressively depleted while the region ahead remains at its initial high concentration. The peak height stays approximately constant (~0.70 OD), indicating the wave is close to a travelling-wave steady state.

Result 5 — Antibiotic extension (Step 5)

Suicidal chemotaxis disrupts the wave; density-dependent χ is protective

Adding an antibiotic field ahead of the bacterial wave, with bacteria attracted toward it (motivated by suicidal chemotaxis in Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Oliveira et al. 2022), disrupts the wave above the MIC. Bacteria at the leading edge enter the killing zone and are eliminated, slowing or halting wave propagation.

At sub-MIC concentrations the wave is only weakly perturbed. Importantly, density-dependent χ provides partial protection at high density: crowded bacteria are less strongly attracted to the antibiotic, so fewer enter the killing zone. The same mechanism that broadens the wave at high density also inadvertently reduces suicidal antibiotic attraction.

Time evolution showing the bacterial band (green) eroding as it reaches the antibiotic zone (red).
