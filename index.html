Introduction

Chemotaxis is a fundamental mechanism by which motile bacteria navigate their environment in response to chemical gradients. In Escherichia coli, this process enables cells to move toward nutrients (chemoattractants) and away from harmful conditions (chemorepellents), helping them survive in changing environments (The Molecular Switch | Princeton University Press, 2020). At the single-cell level, chemotaxis is controlled by a signalling pathway involving methyl-accepting chemotaxis proteins (MCPs), the kinase CheA, and the response regulator CheY. Changes in chemical concentration alter flagellar rotation, producing alternating “runs” and “tumbles” that bias movement toward favourable conditions. Because bacteria are too small to sense spatial gradients directly, they rely on temporal comparisons, adjusting their movement based on whether conditions are improving or worsening. This response is further refined by receptor methylation, which allows cells to adapt to persistent signals and remain sensitive to new changes (Hadjidemetriou et al., 2022).

At the population level, chemotaxis is commonly described using the Keller–Segel model, which captures collective behaviours such as aggregation and travelling bands (Keller & Segel, 1971). However, this classical framework assumes constant chemotactic sensitivity and does not account for changes in behaviour at higher cell densities. Experimental work has shown that bacterial responses become nonlinear under crowded conditions due to factors such as receptor saturation and resource depletion (Phan et al. 2024).

In natural environments, bacteria are rarely exposed to nutrients alone. Instead, they encounter mixtures of beneficial and harmful compounds, including antibiotics produced by competing microbes. These conditions link bacterial movement directly to both nutrient acquisition and survival as accessing resources may also involve exposure to stress. This has been experimentally shown by Oliveira et al. (2022), who showed that bacteria actively move toward antibiotics rather than away from them. It occurs only when the antibiotic concentration exceeds a threshold (the minimum inhibitory concentration or MIC), and the movement bias increases with the gradient strength. Eventually, cells that migrate into high-antibiotic regions lose viability, leading to what is called “suicidal chemotaxis”.

Our project aims to develop a mathematical model that integrates classical chemotaxis modelling, density-dependent corrections, and an antibiotic-driven extension.

Numerical Method

The PDEs are solved using explicit Euler time-stepping with central finite differences. Grid size: N = 300, dz ≈ 0.02 mm.

dt < dz² / (2 · max(Db, DA))

No-flux boundary conditions are applied at both ends.

Methods

Experimental data

We used the processed dataset from Phan et al. (2024), which contains mean bacterial density profiles B(z) and aspartate concentration profiles A(z) for four density groups: LOW (~0.1-0.3 OD), MID (~3.0-3.3 OD), HIGH (~6.2-6.7 OD), and EXT (~7.6-8.3 OD). The coordinate z is in the co-moving frame, so z = 0 always marks the peak of the bacterial band.

Model fitting

Parameters for the original KS model were fitted to the LOW density group. Parameters for the modified model were fitted simultaneously to all four density groups. The rate-of-change diagnostic compared the model right-hand side against the measured temporal derivative of bacterial density, providing a direct test of each term in the PDE.

Forward simulation

The full coupled PDE system was integrated forward in time using explicit Euler time-stepping on a 300-point spatial grid (dz = 0.02 mm). Initial conditions were taken from the experimentally measured LOW density profiles, interpolated onto the coarser simulation grid. No-flux boundary conditions were applied at both ends of the channel.

Antibiotic extension

The antibiotic field was initialised as a Gaussian patch ahead of the bacterial band. Parameters for the antibiotic chemotaxis and killing terms were chosen to be consistent with the order-of-magnitude estimates from Oliveira et al. (2022). The MIC was set to 0.70 µM.

Results

Figure 1

Classical Keller-Segel model reproduces chemotactic band formation but fails at high density

We first simulated the classical Keller-Segel (KS) model to evaluate its ability to reproduce experimentally observed bacterial distributions across four density regimes (LOW, MID, HIGH, EXT). The experimental data showed a clear density-dependent trend: as initial bacterial density increased, the peak bacterial density at the band front rose sharply, reaching OD approximately 5 in the EXT group, while the aspartate gradient steepened as higher-density populations depleted nutrient more rapidly behind the advancing front.

At low density, the model closely matched the data. The bacterial peak was broad and low-amplitude, and the rate-of-change profiles were small and roughly symmetric, reflecting weak chemotactic flux. As density increased the cracks started to show. At HIGH and EXT densities, the model significantly overestimated both the magnitude and sharpness of the chemotactic response. In the EXT group specifically, the predicted rate-of-change peak was roughly twice the observed value. This makes it clear that constant chemotactic sensitivity is not a reasonable assumption once bacterial density gets high.

Rate-of-change diagnostic comparing model prediction against measured data across all four density groups.

Figure 2

Density-dependent modification improves agreement across all regimes

To fix this, we introduced a density-dependent chemotactic sensitivity alongside a saturating nutrient consumption term. As bacterial density increases, crowding and receptor saturation reduce how strongly individual cells respond to a gradient, and resource uptake becomes limited. This is consistent with what Phan et al. (2024) observed experimentally.

The modification worked well. Across all four density groups, the modified model matched both the amplitude and spatial structure of the measured rate-of-change curves. At LOW density the change was minimal, as expected, since corrections are negligible when density is far below the half-saturation threshold. At EXT density the improvement was substantial: the original model's sharp overshoot was corrected, and the modified model stayed within the experimental standard deviation across the full spatial range. A single density-dependent correction was enough to bring the model into agreement across nearly two orders of magnitude in bacterial density.

Figure 3

Simulations reproduce travelling wave propagation and self-generated attractant depletion

Running full time-dependent simulations of the modified model, initialised from LOW-density experimental profiles, reproduced the travelling chemotactic band. Bacteria that were initially broadly distributed aggregated into a moving peak that shifted roughly 2 mm along the z-axis over nine minutes. Behind the band, aspartate was progressively depleted, while a steep gradient was maintained ahead of it. This is a self-generated gradient: the bacteria are not just following a pre-existing chemical field, they are actively shaping it by consuming attractant locally, which sustains the directional bias driving forward motion. Band width and speed were consistent with experimental observations.

Figure 4

Antibiotic extension introduces competing chemotactic signals

We then extended the model to include an antibiotic field, with a chemotactic sensitivity term that only switches on above the minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC), and a killing term that increases with antibiotic exposure. Early on, bacteria behaved as before, following the nutrient gradient and forming a travelling band. But as the band reached regions where antibiotic concentration exceeded the MIC, the competing signal took over, pulling bacteria toward the antibiotic source. Over ten minutes, the bacterial peak drifted into the high-antibiotic zone, aspartate was simultaneously depleted, and in regions where antibiotics stayed above the MIC, bacterial density collapsed. The antibiotic field itself decayed over time due to bacterial degradation. These simulations capture the core features of suicidal chemotaxis described by Oliveira et al. (2022), and show that a relatively small positive antibiotic chemotactic sensitivity is enough to redirect population-level movement once the antibiotic gradient becomes steep enough.

We developed an interactive, browser-based simulation that allows real-time variation of key parameters, including food attraction (χ_f), antibiotic attraction (χ_a), antibiotic-induced death rate, MIC threshold, and initial bacterial density.

Across simulations, three distinct regimes emerged depending on the relative strength of attraction and toxicity.

At low antibiotic attraction and/or high death rates, bacterial populations remained confined to nutrient-rich regions. Cells aggregated into stable clusters around food sources, with minimal penetration into antibiotic zones. The MIC contour acted as a soft exclusion boundary, beyond which population density remained negligible.

At intermediate parameter values, bacteria were drawn toward antibiotic regions but accumulated at the MIC boundary. This produced ring-like high-density structures at the edge of toxic zones, indicating a balance between chemotactic drift and antibiotic-induced mortality.

When antibiotic attraction dominated over death rate, bacterial populations crossed the MIC threshold and migrated into high-antibiotic regions. This resulted in rapid population collapse, consistent with the suicidal chemotaxis dynamics observed in the continuum model (Fig. 5).

In addition, increasing the initial population size enhanced aggregation strength and accelerated migration toward both nutrient and antibiotic sources. However, higher densities also amplified effective exposure to antibiotics near the MIC boundary, leading to sharper population declines in collapse regimes.

Discussion

The classical Keller–Segel model captures basic features such as band formation (Keller & Segel, 1971), but its limitations become clear at higher densities. As bacterial density increases, the assumption of constant chemotactic sensitivity breaks down, and responses become weaker and more regulated. This is consistent with experimental findings showing that bacterial chemotaxis becomes nonlinear at high densities due to crowding and receptor saturation (Phan et al., 2024).

The most interesting result comes from the presence of antibiotics. Instead of avoiding harmful conditions, bacteria can move toward antibiotic gradients and enter regions where they cannot survive. This behaviour, known as suicidal chemotaxis, has been observed experimentally and suggests that bacterial sensing is not always aligned with survival outcomes (Oliveira et al., 2022). It also indicates that antibiotics in natural environments may act as cues linked to microbial competition, rather than purely as lethal agents (Linares et al., 2006).

These findings have broader implications for understanding bacterial behaviour in real environments. Bacteria are often exposed to both nutrients and antibiotics simultaneously, meaning their movement reflects a balance between growth and survival. In this sense, bacterial diet is not determined only by nutrient availability, but also by whether accessing those nutrients exposes them to harmful conditions.

This is especially relevant for the rising problem of antimicrobial resistance. If bacteria actively move toward antibiotic-rich regions, this changes how populations are exposed to antibiotics and how selection pressures act on them. Spatial exposure to antibiotics has been shown to influence bacterial survival and adaptation (Hermsen et al., 2012), and movement-driven exposure may further shape these dynamics.

Overall, this work shows that chemotaxis is not simply movement toward food, but a process influenced by density, environmental structure, and competing signals. Understanding this helps connect bacterial movement, diet, and health, and provides insight into how bacterial populations behave in complex and realistic environments.
